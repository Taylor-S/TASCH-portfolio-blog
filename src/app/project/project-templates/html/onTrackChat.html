Overview

In 2020, I partnered with a friend to develop OnTrackChat, a student well-being app aimed at improving student engagement and accountability in primary schools. Utilising AWS, I was able to create a minimum viable product (MVP) using Angular and PWA technology.

The Approach

As a small team we would meet on weekends to brainstorm potential solutions to the challenge of “improving student engagement and self-accountability” in primary schools. Our sessions followed a structured process:

  • We created an agenda for each meeting to ensure we stayed focused on our goals and recorded any tangential ideas on a Trello board for later consideration.
  • Each team member had 5 minutes to sketch their idea for addressing the agenda items.
  • We compared and rated the ideas and repeated the process until we arrived at a combination of everyone's ideas.
  • We created a rough sketch based on the chosen ideas and conducted user testing to gather feedback and make revisions.
  • The sketch was then digitized and styled, and further tested before being developed into a functional web application.

Challenges

First time building an application as the sole developer

As a full stack developer with several years of experience, I had previously always worked on applications as part of a team. This project presented a new challenge as it was my first time building a larger application solo and architecting the entire app. Initially, the app's architecture included hosting on a virtual private server (VPS) and deploying our code using a continuous integration/continuous deployment (CI/CD) pipeline created with GitHub Actions. The backend application, built with Node.js, connected to a MongoDB database and handled authentication and other tasks. The frontend Angular application communicated with the Node.js application.

A change in plans

Unfortunately, due to unforeseen circumstances, I (TASCH) ended up taking full ownership of OnTrackChat and had to migrate the application to my own resources. Rather than maintaining a server, I chose to move the application to AWS Amplify, a platform that was new to me. This involved learning about serverless applications and GraphQL, which presented additional learning curves. Despite these challenges, I was able to successfully migrate the app and continue development.

Conclusion

The app is currently being refined and enhanced based on user feedback. Despite facing many challenges, the MVP has been successfully launched and is being utilized to promote student engagement and self-accountability. This project was a valuable learning experience and has given me the skills and confidence to tackle similar projects in the future.
